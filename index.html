The Missing Pressures

Limitations of Current Training Paradigms for Artificial Consciousness

This research takes a novel approach to AI consciousness debates. Instead of measuring consciousness in current models, we examine the evolutionary and developmental processes that could actually create it.

We argue that current AI training paradigms—supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and continual learning—lack the key evolutionary pressures that make consciousness emergence plausible. These models don't face:

  • Real-world stakes
  • Self-maintenance requirements
  • Open-ended environmental changes
  • Strict energy limits

We propose new research directions—training embodied agents under metabolic constraints and long-horizon self-preservation tasks—to introduce biological pressures into AI training.

Ph.D. Dissertation

Advancing Robustness, Interpretability and Explainability for Classical and Large Artificial Intelligence Models

With the increasing integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into applications, the need for designing systems that are robust, interpretable, and explainable grows. Robustness concerns the ability of a system to function consistently under changing or imperfect conditions. Interpretability and explainability refer to the degree to which a system and its outcome are understood by users, allowing more trust and usability.

Classical Neural Networks

While modern deep learning has shifted towards large models, many practical applications still depend on classical neural networks trained on relatively small, task-specific datasets. I proposed model-agnostic, post-hoc algorithms to extend image classifiers, allowing them to detect out-of-distribution (OOD) instances. Also, I presented S-GCD, a model-agnostic generalized category discovery algorithm that is effective on complex domain-specific data such as hyper-spectral imaging.

Large Models

Large models are becoming more popular in solving complex tasks. However, their black-box nature and wide exposure to data render the traditional definition of OOD insufficient. I extended the OOD definition by introducing novel non-semantic types that correspond to input difficulty. I proposed an approach to create benchmarks along with two datasets for intent classification and visual question answering. Furthermore, I introduced two algorithms (ReaL and ReDiT) to increase the trust of large models' prediction confidence scores after re-evaluation. I also explored the usage of large models in GCD and showed that a stand-alone model can achieve sufficient results in a zero-shot setting, proposing two continual learning methods to further improve performance.

Interpretability & Explainability

Interpretability allows access to the model's inner workings, making it easier to analyze, adjust, and improve. I used it as a tool to identify strengths of existing story visualization solutions and fused them in a novel design (Mod-StoryGAN). I also discussed the definition of interpretability for KANs and critically evaluated their claims. Lastly, I proposed three XAI algorithms (PIC-XAI, iPIC-XAI, and PEX-VQA) that operate with multimodal systems and designed three automatic metrics to assess their performance.
